# study-of-iguana-poop
A field-friendly, educational study of iguana scat (droppings, feces) — serious science with a playful nose for natural history.

---

## Overview
This repository collects notes, images, and field-guides related to the morphology and ecological information that can be inferred from iguana droppings. It is intended for educators, citizen scientists, field biologists, and natural-history enthusiasts who want a structured, ethical approach to observing and documenting iguana scat.

What you’ll find here:
- High-level morphology notes to help you classify field observations.
- Clues for inferring diet and habitat use from scat.
- Ethics and safety guidance so we study responsibly.
- An annotated image gallery for visual comparison.
- A roadmap for future work and data-sharing.

---

## Why it matters
- **Ecosystem insights:** Scat reveals diet composition, seed dispersal roles, and nutrient distribution by iguanas.
- **Non-invasive monitoring:** Observational scat studies allow population- and community-level inferences without stressing animals.
- **Education & outreach:** Accessible material for schools and citizen-science programs to engage people in field biology.
- **Conservation value:** Scat-based data can inform habitat use, human-wildlife interactions, and invasive-species impacts.

## Morphology notes
A quick reference for describing and categorizing iguana scat in the field. Use consistent units and a photographic scale when possible.

- **Size & shape**
  - Range: small pellets to long tubular deposits depending on species, age, and diet.
  - Note cross-section (cylindrical, segmented, amorphous) and approximate length/diameter.
- **Color**
  - Fresh: typically greenish to brown for herbivorous species, darker for mixed diets.
  - White or chalky portions are usually **urate** (uric acid), common in reptile droppings.
- **Texture & consistency**
  - Firm, fibrous (high plant-fiber diets) vs. mucous/soft (possible fruit or high-water diets).
  - Presence of liquid surfaces may indicate recent feeding on succulent plants.
- **Composition**
  - Visible plant parts: leaves, stems, seeds, flower fragments.
  - Animal matter: insects, small vertebrate remains (species-dependent; rare).
  - Mineral grit or shell fragments (ingested for digestion/grit).
- **Odor**
  - Varies; note strength as fresh vs. aged — useful for time-since-deposition estimates.
- **Parasite indicators**
  - Presence of worms or unusual textures may warrant follow-up by a licensed vet or lab (do not attempt extraction in the field).

Record: GPS, date/time, habitat notes, collector/observer name, and a scale in photos.

## Habitat / diet clues
Use scat to infer recent feeding and habitat use; combine with behavioral and site observations.

- **Plant-fragment types**
  - Long fibrous pieces → leafy diets (e.g., foliage browsers).
  - Seeds and intact fruit parts → frugivory and potential seed dispersal; note seed species for plant–iguana interactions.
- **Algal matter**
  - Dark, slimy bits often indicate marine/algal feeding (notable in marine iguanas).
- **Insect fragments**
  - Small exoskeleton pieces suggest opportunistic insect consumption (more common in juveniles or in certain locales).
- **Sand/grit**
  - Heavy mineral content can indicate feeding along beaches or rocky shores.
- **Spatial clues**
  - Repeated deposits under perches or basking sites mark home-range centers or territorial boundaries.

Always corroborate scat-derived hypotheses with vegetation surveys and direct observation when possible.

## Ethics & safety
We prioritize animal welfare, legal compliance, and personal safety. Follow these best practices.

- **Do no harm**
  - Never handle, harass, or capture iguanas to obtain scat samples. Observe from a distance and collect only material that is already detached from the animal.
- **Permits & local laws**
  - Obtain necessary permits for sample collection, photography, or exporting data/specimens. Check local wildlife regulations and protected-species legislation.
- **Biosecurity & PPE**
  - Use disposable gloves when handling samples. Avoid direct contact with mucous membranes. Wash hands thoroughly after fieldwork.
  - Decontaminate tools and surfaces after use to prevent pathogen transfer between sites.
- **Sample handling**
  - Prefer photographic documentation and non-invasive sampling. If physical samples are collected, store and label them securely and follow chain-of-custody and biosafety guidelines appropriate for your institution.
- **Respect people & property**
  - Ask permission before accessing private land. Be mindful of cultural sensitivities regarding wildlife and local practices.
- **Reporting**
  - If you find evidence of disease, injured animals, or protected species interaction, report to local wildlife authorities or a qualified veterinarian.

## Future work
Planned directions and opportunities for collaboration.

- Standardized field datasheets and mobile-friendly forms for citizen scientists.
- GIS mapping of scat occurrences to identify feeding hotspots and movement corridors.
- Non-invasive laboratory analyses: diet residue identification via microscopy, stable isotope summaries (coarse insights), and seed viability assays — carried out by accredited labs with appropriate permits.
- Parasite and pathogen surveillance in partnership with vets and universities (ethically and legally permitted).
- Educational modules for schools: “Iguana Ecology from Scat” curriculum and workshop materials.
- Open dataset releases with anonymized location data where necessary to protect sensitive populations.

Contributions welcome: open an issue or pull request with data, images, or suggested additions.
